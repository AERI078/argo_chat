Arabian Sea Dynamics

The Arabian Sea is one of the most dynamic regions of the Indian Ocean. Its circulation and ocean conditions are strongly controlled by the monsoon system.

Monsoon Influence
During the southwest monsoon (June–September), strong winds blow from the southwest toward India. These winds:
- Drive surface currents
- Enhance evaporation
- Cause coastal upwelling
- Cool some coastal regions through the rise of deeper water

During the northeast monsoon (winter), wind patterns reverse, causing changes in surface circulation and vertical mixing.

Sea Surface Temperature and Salinity
The Arabian Sea is generally warmer and saltier than the Bay of Bengal because:
- Evaporation is very high
- Rainfall is lower than over the Bay of Bengal
- River input is relatively small

Despite coastal cooling during upwelling, large parts of the Arabian Sea maintain high sea surface temperatures, which help fuel atmospheric convection and influence monsoon rainfall.

Upwelling Zones
Major upwelling regions occur:
- Off the coast of Oman
- Along the Somali coast
- Off the Kerala coast of India

Upwelling brings cold, nutrient-rich deep water to the surface. This supports intense phytoplankton growth and productive fisheries.

Oxygen Minimum Zones (OMZs)
The Arabian Sea contains one of the world's strongest oxygen minimum zones. Between roughly 200 and 1000 meters depth, oxygen concentrations become extremely low.

The OMZ forms because:
- High biological productivity produces large amounts of sinking organic matter
- Microbial decomposition consumes oxygen
- Stratification limits oxygen replenishment from the surface

These low-oxygen conditions strongly affect marine ecosystems and nitrogen cycling.

Importance for Indian Climate
The Arabian Sea strongly affects:
- Indian monsoon rainfall
- Cyclone formation
- Moisture transport into the Indian subcontinent
- Marine productivity and fisheries

Changes in Arabian Sea temperature and circulation can influence extreme rainfall events and regional climate variability.
