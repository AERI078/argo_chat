Salinity and Ocean Circulation

Salinity is the amount of dissolved salts in seawater. It is usually expressed in PSU (Practical Salinity Units). Average ocean salinity is about 35 PSU.

Why Salinity Varies
Salinity changes because of:
- Evaporation
- Precipitation
- River runoff
- Sea ice formation and melting
- Ocean circulation

High evaporation increases salinity because water leaves the ocean surface while salts remain behind. Heavy rainfall or river discharge lowers salinity by adding freshwater.

Halocline
A halocline is a layer where salinity changes rapidly with depth. It is similar to a thermocline, but based on salinity rather than temperature.

Strong haloclines reduce vertical mixing because water density changes rapidly across the layer.

Salinity and Density
Salinity strongly affects seawater density:
- Saltier water is denser
- Fresher water is less dense

Together with temperature, salinity controls ocean stratification and circulation.

Thermohaline Circulation
Global ocean circulation is partly driven by differences in temperature (“thermo”) and salinity (“haline”). Dense water sinks in some regions and helps drive deep ocean circulation across the world ocean.

This circulation redistributes:
- Heat
- Carbon
- Nutrients
- Dissolved gases

Arabian Sea vs Bay of Bengal
The Arabian Sea is generally more saline because:
- Evaporation is high
- Rainfall is lower
- River discharge is limited

The Bay of Bengal has lower salinity because:
- Major rivers such as the Ganga and Brahmaputra deliver large freshwater input
- Rainfall is intense during the monsoon

These salinity differences strongly influence:
- Surface stratification
- Mixed layer depth
- Cyclone intensity
- Monsoon-related ocean dynamics
